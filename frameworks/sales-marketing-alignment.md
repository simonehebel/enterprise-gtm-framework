# Sales and Marketing Alignment Framework

## Purpose

This framework creates shared priorities, responsibilities and performance expectations between Sales and Marketing.

The goal is not only better collaboration, but a coordinated go-to-market operating model that supports account engagement, pipeline development and revenue outcomes.

## When to Use This Framework

Use this framework when an organization needs to:

- improve cooperation between Sales and Marketing,
- align teams around shared markets, segments and accounts,
- establish consistent qualification criteria,
- improve lead and account follow-up,
- clarify ownership across the customer journey,
- connect marketing activities with pipeline outcomes,
- create a shared performance review process.

## Shared Business Outcomes

Sales and Marketing should align around measurable business outcomes such as:

- target-account engagement,
- qualified demand,
- opportunity creation,
- pipeline progression,
- deal acceleration,
- customer expansion,
- revenue contribution.

Activity metrics such as impressions, clicks or event attendance can support analysis, but should not be treated as the final business outcome.

## 1. Market and Account Priorities

Both teams should agree on:

- priority markets,
- priority customer segments,
- strategic accounts,
- relevant industries,
- growth opportunities,
- investment criteria.

Questions to consider:

- Which markets and segments are most important?
- Which accounts require coordinated investment?
- What evidence supports the priorities?
- Which opportunities should not receive additional investment?

## 2. Customer Journey Alignment

Sales and Marketing should share a common understanding of the customer journey.

Define:

- relevant buying stages,
- customer needs at each stage,
- required content and interactions,
- ownership of each interaction,
- signals that indicate progression.

Example stages:

1. Awareness
2. Problem recognition
3. Solution exploration
4. Evaluation
5. Commercial decision
6. Adoption and expansion

## 3. Qualification Criteria

Both teams should agree on what qualifies a lead, account or opportunity for follow-up.

Possible criteria include:

- strategic account fit,
- relevant business need,
- engagement level,
- buying role,
- timing,
- budget signals,
- sales readiness.

Questions to define:

- What is a qualified lead?
- What is an engaged account?
- When should Sales follow up?
- When should an account remain in nurture?
- How is feedback returned to Marketing?

## 4. Roles and Responsibilities

| Area | Marketing | Sales | Shared |
|---|---|---|---|
| Market insight | Lead | Contribute | Review |
| Segment selection | Contribute | Contribute | Approve |
| Account prioritization | Support | Lead | Review |
| Messaging | Lead | Validate | Refine |
| Campaign activation | Lead | Support | Coordinate |
| Lead qualification | Lead | Validate | Improve |
| Opportunity progression | Support | Lead | Review |
| Performance analysis | Lead | Contribute | Decide |
| Investment priorities | Recommend | Recommend | Approve |

The exact ownership model should reflect the organization, Sales motion and customer journey.

## 5. Lead and Account Handover

A structured handover process should define:

- which information is transferred,
- who receives the lead or account,
- expected response time,
- required follow-up actions,
- feedback status,
- recycling criteria.

Example handover information:

- customer or account name,
- segment,
- engagement history,
- content consumed,
- business topic,
- relevant buying role,
- recommended next action.

## 6. Operating Rhythm

Recommended collaboration structure:

### Weekly

- active campaign review,
- priority-account updates,
- follow-up issues,
- immediate actions.

### Monthly

- pipeline contribution,
- conversion performance,
- account engagement,
- program optimization.

### Quarterly

- market and segment priorities,
- strategic-account selection,
- budget allocation,
- GTM performance,
- required changes.

Every meeting should end with:

- decisions,
- actions,
- owners,
- deadlines.

## 7. Shared Performance Indicators

Possible shared metrics include:

- target-account engagement,
- buying committee coverage,
- qualified leads,
- qualified accounts,
- opportunity creation,
- lead-to-opportunity conversion,
- opportunity progression,
- pipeline contribution,
- deal velocity,
- closed-won revenue.

Metrics should reflect the business model and Sales cycle.

## 8. Feedback Loop

Sales feedback should help Marketing improve:

- targeting,
- messaging,
- content,
- qualification,
- timing,
- campaign design.

Marketing insights should help Sales improve:

- account prioritization,
- customer understanding,
- buying signal interpretation,
- follow-up relevance,
- stakeholder engagement.

## Alignment Checklist

Before launching a GTM program, confirm:

- [ ] Priority market is agreed
- [ ] Target segment is agreed
- [ ] Strategic accounts are identified
- [ ] Customer need is documented
- [ ] Value proposition is validated
- [ ] Sales follow-up process is defined
- [ ] Qualification criteria are agreed
- [ ] Roles and responsibilities are clear
- [ ] Shared KPIs are defined
- [ ] Review cadence is scheduled

## Key Principle

Sales and Marketing alignment is not a meeting schedule.

It is a shared operating model that connects:

- customer priorities,
- account decisions,
- responsibilities,
- execution,
- performance measurement.
