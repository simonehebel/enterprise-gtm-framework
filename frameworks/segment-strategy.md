# Segment Strategy Framework

## Purpose

This framework helps translate broad market opportunities into differentiated go-to-market strategies for specific customer groups.

## When to Use This Framework

Use this framework when an organization needs to:

- prioritize customer segments,
- adapt messaging to different customer needs,
- define differentiated GTM motions,
- align Sales and Marketing around target audiences,
- improve the relevance of campaigns and content,
- determine which segments justify focused investment.

## Segment Definition

A useful customer segment should be:

- identifiable,
- commercially relevant,
- reachable,
- sufficiently distinct,
- measurable,
- actionable.

## Segment Assessment

For each segment, assess the following dimensions.

### 1. Customer Profile

Define the relevant organizational characteristics.

Questions to consider:

- What company size is relevant?
- Which industries should be included?
- Which geographies matter?
- What business model does the customer use?
- Which technologies or systems are already in place?
- What is the organization's level of maturity?

### 2. Business Priorities

Identify the strategic context of the segment.

Questions to consider:

- Which business objectives are most important?
- Which transformation pressures exist?
- What operational challenges must be addressed?
- Which growth priorities create urgency?
- Which risks influence decision-making?

### 3. Buying Context

Understand how purchasing decisions are made.

Questions to consider:

- Who is part of the buying committee?
- Who owns the budget?
- Who has decision authority?
- What does the procurement process look like?
- How long is the expected Sales cycle?
- What are the most common objections?

### 4. Customer Need

Define the problem that creates demand.

Questions to consider:

- What problem is the customer trying to solve?
- What is the business impact of the problem?
- How urgent is the need?
- What happens if the customer does nothing?
- Which evidence confirms the need?

### 5. Value Proposition

Define why the solution is relevant.

Questions to consider:

- Which business outcome can be enabled?
- What makes the solution differentiated?
- Which proof points are required?
- Which customer language should be used?
- Which claims should be avoided?

### 6. GTM Approach

Define the most appropriate commercial motion.

Questions to consider:

- Which Sales motion is suitable?
- Which marketing programs are required?
- Is a 1:1, 1:Few or 1:Many ABM approach appropriate?
- What role should partners play?
- Which content is required?
- Which channels are most effective?

## Segment Strategy Statement

Use the following structure:

> For [target segment], which is facing [priority problem], we provide [relevant value] by enabling [business outcome]. Our GTM approach will focus on [primary motion], supported by [evidence, content and channels].

## Example

> For large industrial enterprises undergoing digital transformation, which face fragmented processes and limited operational transparency, we provide a structured approach to identifying improvement opportunities and enabling measurable business outcomes. Our GTM approach will combine targeted account engagement, executive-level content and close Sales and Marketing coordination.

## Required Decisions

For every segment, document:

- strategic priority,
- customer need,
- business value,
- buying committee,
- preferred GTM motion,
- relevant messages,
- required content,
- success metrics,
- accountable owner.

## Key Principle

Not every customer should receive the same message, content or GTM approach.

A strong segment strategy connects:

- customer context,
- business need,
- value proposition,
- buying process,
- execution model.
